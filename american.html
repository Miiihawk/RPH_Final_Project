American Colonial Period

1898 - Treaty of Paris

The Spanish-American War concludes with the signing of the Treaty of Paris, under which Spain ceded the Philippines, Puerto Rico, and Guam to the United States for $20 million. Filipinos, who expected independence after helping defeat Spanish forces, feel deeply betrayed. This treaty marks the start of U.S. sovereignty over the Philippines and sparks significant anti-American sentiments.

1899–1902 - Philippine-American War

Tensions escalate into the Philippine-American War, with Filipino forces under Emilio Aguinaldo adopting guerrilla tactics against American troops. The war resulted in over 200,000 Filipino deaths, including civilians, primarily from famine and disease. The capture of Aguinaldo in 1901 symbolizes the formal end of major resistance, but uprisings continue among different groups, including in Samar and Batangas.

1901 - Establishment of Civil Government

After Aguinaldo’s capture, the U.S. establishes a civil government in the Philippines, appointing William Howard Taft as the first Governor-General. Taft focuses on infrastructure, public education, and economic development while asserting U.S. sovereignty. The elite’s cooperation with colonial authorities becomes critical in this phase.

1902 - Philippine Organic Act

This act creates the Philippine Commission as a legislative body and promises Filipino self-governance eventually. It provides limited political representation and establishes a framework for American-style governance, reinforcing the dominance of the elite while marginalizing the masses.

1903–1908 - Public Education System

The U.S. introduces a public education system with English as the medium of instruction, establishing a foundation for American cultural influence. The pensionado program sends Filipino students to study in the U.S., creating a pro-American elite that helps reinforce colonial objectives.

1916 - Jones Act (Philippine Autonomy Act)

The Jones Act serves as a U.S. commitment to eventual Philippine independence once a "stable government" is achieved. It establishes an elected Philippine Senate, significantly increasing Filipino political participation but maintaining American oversight.

1934 - Tydings-McDuffie Act

The Tydings-McDuffie Act sets the stage for the Commonwealth of the Philippines, outlining a 10-year transition period toward independence. It mandates the drafting of a Philippine constitution and provides a timeline for self-rule.

1935 - Commonwealth of the Philippines

The Commonwealth government was inaugurated with Manuel L. Quezon as president. The government emphasizes defense, economic reforms, and education as part of its preparation for full independence.

1941-1945 - Japanese Occupation and World War II

The Japanese invasion of the Philippines during World War II resulted in widespread destruction, including events like the Bataan Death March and the devastation of Manila. Filipino guerrillas, alongside U.S. forces led by General Douglas MacArthur, resist the occupation, culminating in liberation in 1945.

1946 - Philippine Independence

On July 4, 1946, the Philippines gained full independence under the presidency of Manuel Roxas, ending nearly 50 years of American rule. While politically independent, the Philippines remains economically and militarily tied to the United States.
