Japanesee Colonial Period

1941 - The Attack on Pearl Harbor

The Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor on December 7, 1941, marked the United States' entry into World War II, and the Philippines became a crucial battleground. The U.S. Army Forces in the Far East (USAFFE), under General Douglas MacArthur, relied heavily on Filipino troops to defend the islands. Despite their efforts, Japanese forces swiftly advanced.

1942 - The Fall of the Philippines

Japanese forces defeated the USAFFE in the Battle of Bataan, forcing the surrender of over 76,000 American and Filipino troops on April 9, 1942. The captives were subjected to the infamous Bataan Death March, a 60-mile forced march characterized by inhumane conditions, leading to thousands of deaths.

1943 - Occupation and Resistance

Japan established a puppet government led by José P. Laurel, who reluctantly collaborated under duress. Resistance movements flourished, with guerrilla fighters conducting ambushes and espionage. These movements demonstrated widespread Filipino opposition to Japanese rule.

1944 - Return of McArthur

General MacArthur landed in Leyte on October 20, 1944, fulfilling his famous promise, "I shall return." The Battle of Leyte Gulf became a turning point, decisively crippling the Japanese navy and setting the stage for the liberation of the Philippines.

1570 - Liberation and Aftermath

The Battle of Manila in early 1945 led to the liberation of the capital, though it caused massive destruction and civilian casualties. Japan's surrender in September 1945 marked the end of four years of brutal occupation and set the stage for Philippine independence in 1946.

1946 - Philippine Independence

The Philippines officially gained independence on July 4, 1946, through the Treaty of Manila. President Manuel Roxas prioritized post-war recovery but faced economic dependency issues under the Bell Trade Act, which granted Americans equal access to Philippine resources.

1947 - Military Bases Agreement

A 99-year lease was granted to the U.S. for maintaining strategic bases, including Clark Air Base and Subic Naval Base, solidifying American military presence in the region.

1948 - 1953 - Quirino Adminstration

Elpidio Quirino's presidency focused on economic reconstruction, but allegations of corruption marred his administration. The Hukbalahap (Huk) Rebellion escalated during this period, driven by peasant demands for land reform.

1953 - 1957 - Magsaysay Administration

President Ramon Magsaysay prioritized agrarian reform and rural development, effectively addressing the Huk Rebellion through a combination of military action and grassroots initiatives.

1957 – 1961 - Garcia Administration

Carlos P. Garcia introduced the Filipino First Policy, promoting local businesses and cultural nationalism. His administration also implemented austerity measures to stabilize the economy.

1961 - 1965 - Macapagal Administration

Diosdado Macapagal shifted Independence Day to June 12, commemorating the 1898 declaration of independence. His land reform program targeted tenancy issues but faced opposition from landlords.

1965 - 1972 - Marcos Administration

Ferdinand Marcos emphasized infrastructure and military modernization during his first term. However, his second term was marked by allegations of corruption, economic mismanagement, and political unrest.

1972 - Declaration of Martial Law

On September 21, 1972, Marcos declared Martial Law, citing threats from communists and separatists. This marked the end of the Third Republic and the beginning of an authoritarian regime.
